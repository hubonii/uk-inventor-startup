# Non-Customer Analysis: Three Tiers of Untapped Demand

## Framework

Blue Ocean Strategy identifies three tiers of non-customers — people and businesses NOT currently using existing solutions. These non-customers represent far greater growth potential than fighting over existing customers.

In logistics, most platforms fight over the same pool of merchants already using same-day delivery. We target the **vastly larger pool of merchants who DON'T use same-day delivery** — and unlock entirely new use cases that don't exist yet.

## Tier 1: "Soon-to-Be" Non-Customers

### Definition

Merchants who currently use same-day delivery services but are **actively unhappy** with the cost, reliability, or experience. They sit at the edge of the market, ready to leave — or already reducing their usage.

### Profile

These merchants use Stuart, Lalamove, Gophr, or traditional couriers for same-day delivery but:
- Wince at every delivery fee (£6–12 per drop)
- Only use same-day for urgent/high-value items
- Regularly decide "it's not worth the delivery fee" and tell customers to collect
- Have considered stopping same-day delivery entirely
- Complain about inconsistent courier quality

### Segment Examples

| Merchant Type | Current Spend | Pain Point | Switch Trigger |
|--------------|--------------|------------|----------------|
| **Independent pharmacies** | £200–500/month on Stuart | £7–9 per prescription delivery eats into margins | Same delivery at £4–5 saves £100+/month |
| **Print shops / Copy centers** | £150–300/month | Urgent document delivery is core business but margins are thin | Lower cost = more deliveries = more print orders |
| **Boutique retailers** | £100–400/month | Same-day delivery offered to compete with Amazon, but cost is unsustainable | 40% cost reduction makes same-day delivery viable as standard offering |
| **Florists** | £300–600/month | High delivery frequency, time-sensitive product | Lower per-delivery cost dramatically improves margins |
| **Pet shops** | £100–200/month | Heavy items (pet food) require delivery but margins are razor-thin | Cost reduction makes delivery of low-margin items viable |

### Size Estimate

**UCL/Bloomsbury Wedge:**
- ~120 independent retail merchants in the zone
- Estimated 25–35 currently using some form of same-day delivery
- Of those, ~20 (60–70%) are unhappy with current cost/service
- **Tier 1 target: 20 merchants in immediate zone**

**Greater London (scaling estimate):**
- ~15,000 independent merchants using same-day delivery across London
- ~10,000 unhappy with cost (industry NPS for delivery platforms is -15 to +5)
- **Tier 1 London total: ~10,000 merchants**

**UK-wide (long-term):**
- ~50,000 independent merchants using same-day delivery
- ~30,000 unhappy with current providers
- **Tier 1 UK total: ~30,000 merchants**

### Conversion Strategy

**Approach: Direct cost comparison + risk-free trial**

```
CONVERSION FUNNEL — TIER 1:

1. IDENTIFY: Map all merchants in wedge currently using delivery services
   (Look for Stuart/Lalamove branded bags, ask directly)

2. APPROACH: Student courier visits merchant, shows cost comparison:
   "You're paying £7.50 per delivery with Stuart.
    Same delivery with us: £4.37. That's £3.13 saved per drop."

3. CALCULATE: Help merchant calculate monthly savings:
   "You do ~40 deliveries/month? That's £125/month saved."

4. TRIAL: Offer first month free (no £49 subscription):
   "Try 10 deliveries free. No commitment. Cancel anytime."

5. CONVERT: After trial, merchant sees real savings on dashboard:
   "You saved £X this month. Want to continue?"

Expected conversion rate: 40-50% (strong value proposition, low switching cost)
```

### Barriers to Conversion

| Barrier | Severity | Mitigation |
|---------|----------|------------|
| **Incumbent contracts** | Medium | Most gig platform contracts are month-to-month; no lock-in |
| **Integration effort** | Low | Dashboard requires zero integration; API is optional |
| **Trust in new platform** | High | First-month free trial eliminates financial risk; TOTP QR demo builds confidence |
| **Courier availability fears** | High | Must ensure 5-min courier assignment in wedge before approaching merchants |
| **"Never heard of you"** | High | University partnership and local press coverage build initial credibility |

## Tier 2: "Refusing" Non-Customers

### Definition

Merchants who have **consciously evaluated** same-day delivery and **rejected it** as too expensive, too complex, or unnecessary for their business. They know same-day delivery exists but have decided it's "not for them."

### Profile

These merchants:
- Have never signed up for any delivery platform
- Tell customers "sorry, we don't deliver — you'll need to come collect"
- Believe delivery is only for big retailers and online shops
- Have looked at Stuart/Lalamove pricing and decided it's not worth it
- May offer informal "I'll drop it round after closing" service to loyal customers

### Segment Examples

| Merchant Type | Why They Refuse | What Changes Their Mind |
|--------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Corner shops / newsagents** | "My average transaction is £8. A £7 delivery fee would double the price." | At £3–4 delivery, adding delivery to a £15 basket becomes viable |
| **Hardware stores** | "We sell nails and screws for £2. Nobody's paying £7 delivery for that." | Aggregated orders: "Your £2 nails + £15 drill bit + £8 sandpaper = £25 basket with £4 delivery" |
| **Dry cleaners** | "We've never offered delivery. Customers come to us." | Student couriers pick up and deliver — new revenue stream for the dry cleaner |
| **Key cutters / shoe repair** | "Our service requires the customer to be present for fitting." | Pickup service: courier collects shoes, brings them back repaired |
| **Bakeries / delis** | "Our margins are 30%. Delivery fees would eat all profit." | At £3–4 delivery on a £12 lunch order, delivery adds revenue not cost |
| **Bookshops** | "Amazon already offers free delivery. We can't compete." | Same-day local delivery of a book within 30 minutes — Amazon can't do that |
| **Art supply stores** | "Our customers browse and choose in person." | Restock orders: art students reorder supplies they know by name |

### Size Estimate

**UCL/Bloomsbury Wedge:**
- ~120 independent retail merchants in the zone
- ~85 do NOT currently offer any delivery service
- Of those, ~50 would consider delivery if cost dropped below £5
- **Tier 2 target: 50 merchants in immediate zone**

**Greater London:**
- ~200,000 independent retail/service businesses
- ~150,000 do not offer delivery
- ~50,000 would consider it at sub-£5 pricing
- **Tier 2 London total: ~50,000 merchants**

**UK-wide:**
- ~500,000 independent retail/service businesses
- ~375,000 do not offer delivery
- ~125,000 would consider at sub-£5 pricing
- **Tier 2 UK total: ~125,000 merchants**

### Conversion Strategy

**Approach: "Delivery is now affordable" education campaign + neighbor effect**

```
CONVERSION FUNNEL — TIER 2:

1. DEMONSTRATE: Show neighboring merchant (Tier 1 convert) success:
   "The pharmacy next door did 40 deliveries last month. 
    Brought in £600 in orders they would have lost to Amazon."

2. EDUCATE: Reframe delivery as revenue generator, not cost:
   "You're not paying for delivery. Your CUSTOMER pays for delivery.
    You're adding a new revenue stream."

3. CALCULATE: Build the business case:
   "If 10% of your walk-in customers used delivery instead,
    at average basket £18 + £4 delivery = £22/order,
    that's 20 extra orders/month = £360 new revenue."

4. SIMPLIFY: Address complexity fears:
   "No app to download. No integration needed. 
    Just log into our dashboard, click 'Dispatch', print a QR label."

5. TRIAL: Free first month, including subscription:
   "Zero cost for 30 days. If you don't like it, cancel. 
    You'll have spent nothing."

Expected conversion rate: 15-25% (harder sell — these merchants have already rejected delivery)
```

### Barriers to Conversion

| Barrier | Severity | Mitigation |
|---------|----------|------------|
| **"Delivery isn't for businesses like mine"** | Very High | Show similar businesses succeeding; local case studies |
| **No delivery packaging/process** | Medium | Provide free starter kit: QR label printer, basic packaging guidance |
| **Customer demand uncertainty** | High | "Try it for a month. If your customers don't use it, cancel." |
| **Technology intimidation** | Medium | Dashboard designed for non-technical users; in-person onboarding help from student ambassadors |
| **"I'm too busy to add another thing"** | High | Emphasize simplicity: "One button. Five seconds. Done." |
| **Perceived competition with Amazon** | Medium | Reframe: "You offer something Amazon can't — local, same-day, personal" |

### Revenue Potential (Tier 2)

| Metric | Per Merchant | 50 Merchants (Wedge) | 50,000 Merchants (London) |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| Monthly subscription | £49 | £2,450 | £2,450,000 |
| Avg. deliveries/month | 20 (lower — new to delivery) | 1,000 | 1,000,000 |
| Platform fee per delivery | £0.57 | £570 | £570,000 |
| **Monthly revenue** | **£60** | **£3,020** | **£3,020,000** |
| **Annual revenue** | **£720** | **£36,240** | **£36,240,000** |

**Critical insight:** Tier 2 merchants generate lower per-merchant revenue (fewer deliveries) but the market is **5x larger** than Tier 1. Total addressable revenue from Tier 2 is **4x greater** than Tier 1.

## Tier 3: "Unexplored" Non-Customers

### Definition

These are not merchants at all — they are **entirely new use cases** that no delivery platform currently serves. These are individuals and groups who have a latent need for local logistics but have never conceptualized it as a "delivery" problem.

### Profile

These non-customers:
- Have never thought about using a delivery platform
- Have needs that current delivery platforms can't serve (too small, too informal, too niche)
- Would use local logistics if it were cheap enough, simple enough, and trustworthy enough
- Represent net-new demand — not stolen from incumbents

### Use Case Examples

#### 3A. Student Textbook & Course Material Exchange

**The need:** University students frequently need to borrow, return, or exchange textbooks, course packs, and study materials. Currently, this happens via in-person meetups arranged through WhatsApp groups or library collection.

**The opportunity:**
- UCL has ~45,000 students
- Average student buys/borrows 5–8 textbooks per year
- Used textbook market at UCL estimated at £200,000+/year
- **Addressable deliveries: 5,000–10,000/year from UCL alone**

**How it works with LLP:**
```
Student A has textbook → Lists on student marketplace (e.g., Vinted, Facebook)
Student B buys/borrows → Requests delivery via LLP
Courier picks up from Student A's hall → Delivers to Student B's lecture hall
Cost: £2–3 (short distance, student courier)
```

**Why this doesn't exist today:** £7+ delivery fees make it cheaper to walk across campus. At £2–3, courier delivery becomes cheaper than the student's time.

---

#### 3B. Document Courier for Freelancers & Small Businesses

**The need:** Freelancers, solicitors, accountants, and small businesses regularly need to send physical documents — contracts requiring wet signatures, original identity documents, legal filings, tax returns.

**The opportunity:**
- Bloomsbury has high density of solicitors, accountants, and creative agencies
- Average small professional firm sends 5–15 urgent documents/month
- Current solution: send a junior employee (expensive) or use Royal Mail Special Delivery (£7–9, next-day not same-day)
- **Addressable deliveries: 2,000–5,000/year from Bloomsbury zone**

**How it works with LLP:**
```
Solicitor needs contract signed → Dispatches via LLP dashboard
Courier picks up from solicitor's office → Delivers to client
Client signs → Returns via another LLP dispatch
TOTP chain-of-custody proves document reached intended recipient
Cost: £4–5 per trip (vs. £7–9 Royal Mail or £20+ using a taxi)
```

**Why this doesn't exist today:** No delivery platform offers cryptographic chain-of-custody for documents. Solicitors need proof of delivery that would stand up in court. Our TOTP handshake provides exactly this — making us the first platform suitable for legal document courier.

---

#### 3C. Local Food Sharing & Community Meal Delivery

**The need:** Community kitchens, religious organizations, food banks, and neighborhood groups regularly prepare food for distribution. Currently, recipients must collect in person, limiting reach.

**The opportunity:**
- UCL area has 5+ community kitchens and food banks
- Each serves 50–200 people/week
- Many elderly or disabled recipients cannot collect in person
- **Addressable deliveries: 3,000–8,000/year from community organizations**

**How it works with LLP:**
```
Community kitchen prepares meals → Dispatches via LLP dashboard
Student couriers deliver to registered recipients within 1km
Cost: £2–3 per delivery (often subsidized by community org or council grant)
Social impact: food reaches people who otherwise go without
```

**Why this doesn't exist today:** Deliveroo/Uber Eats serve restaurants, not community kitchens. Their minimum fees (£3–5 platform + £2–4 delivery) make charitable food delivery uneconomic. At our cost structure, community organizations can afford delivery.

---

#### 3D. Inter-Office Supply Runs (Co-Working Spaces)

**The need:** Bloomsbury has 15+ co-working spaces. Workers in these spaces frequently need supplies — printer paper, adapters, phone chargers, specific stationery — that they've left at home or need from a nearby shop.

**The opportunity:**
- ~5,000 co-working space members in Bloomsbury area
- Average member makes 2–3 "supply runs" per month (leaving workspace to buy something)
- Each supply run takes 20–40 minutes of productive time
- **Addressable deliveries: 5,000–10,000/year from co-working members**

**How it works with LLP:**
```
Worker in WeWork needs phone charger → Opens LLP, finds nearby shop with chargers
Requests delivery → Courier buys and delivers within 30 min
Cost: item price + £3–4 delivery (cheaper than 30 min of lost productivity)
```

---

#### 3E. Return & Repair Logistics

**The need:** Local repair shops (phone repair, tailor, shoe repair, watch repair) require customers to make two trips — drop off and pick up. Many potential customers don't use repair services because of the inconvenience.

**The opportunity:**
- ~30 repair/alteration shops in Bloomsbury zone
- Each could handle 10–20 delivery pickups+returns per week
- **Addressable deliveries: 15,000–30,000/year from repair shops**

**How it works with LLP:**
```
Customer has broken phone screen → Requests LLP pickup from home/office
Courier picks up phone → Delivers to repair shop
Repair completed → Shop dispatches repaired phone back via LLP
Two deliveries per repair: £6–8 total delivery cost
```

### Conversion Strategy (Tier 3)

**Approach: Use-case seeding + community partnerships**

```
CONVERSION FUNNEL — TIER 3:

1. SEED USE CASES: 
   - Partner with UCL student union for textbook exchange
   - Partner with 3 co-working spaces for "supply delivery" service
   - Partner with 2 community kitchens for meal delivery pilot

2. CREATE TEMPLATES:
   - Pre-built "Textbook Exchange" flow in app
   - Pre-built "Document Courier" flow with chain-of-custody emphasis
   - Pre-built "Repair Pickup & Return" flow for repair shops

3. DEMONSTRATE DEMAND:
   - Run 2-week pilots for each use case
   - Measure adoption, repeat usage, and willingness to pay
   - Document case studies with real numbers

4. SCALE WINNERS:
   - Double down on use cases that show >50% repeat usage
   - Deprioritize use cases with <20% repeat usage
   - Build specific marketing for winning use cases

Expected conversion rate: 5-15% (experimental — many use cases won't work)
```

## Strategic Priority

1. **Launch with Tier 1** (unhappy incumbents) — easiest to convert, validates product-market fit
2. **Expand to Tier 2** (refusing non-customers) — largest market, requires education
3. **Experiment with Tier 3** (unexplored use cases) — highest uncertainty, highest potential for category creation
